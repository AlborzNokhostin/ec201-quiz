## Essay 1: Transversality Condition ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê

### Question:
**"Can a country run perpetual current account deficits? Explain using the transversality condition. What does this condition actually require?" (14 marks)**

### Why This Question?
Professor said **"I'll probably put it on the test for sure"** and **"I want everybody to get this right."** This is the most explicitly guaranteed test question.

### Model Answer (14 marks):

**Introduction (2 marks):**
The transversality condition is a key sustainability constraint for international borrowing. A country CAN potentially run perpetual current account deficits, but only if the transversality condition is satisfied.

**The Transversality Condition (4 marks):**
The condition states:
```
lim[t‚Üí‚àû] B_t/(1+r)^t = 0
```

**Crucially, this does NOT mean debt must be zero** (B_t ‚Üí 0). It means the **present discounted value** of debt must go to zero. This is satisfied when:
- Growth rate of debt < Interest rate (g_B < r)

**Intuition (3 marks):**
- If debt grows slower than r, then B_t/(1+r)^t ‚Üí 0 even if B_t ‚Üí ‚àû
- The country must generate sufficient trade balance surpluses to service the debt
- Cannot use "Ponzi scheme" (borrowing to pay interest forever)

**Example (3 marks):**
Suppose r = 5% per year. A country can run CA deficits forever IF:
- The debt grows at, say, 3% per year (g_B = 3% < 5% = r)
- The present value: B_t/(1.05)^t ‚Üí 0 as t ‚Üí ‚àû

**Conclusion (2 marks):**
Yes, perpetual CA deficits are possible under the transversality condition. The key is that debt servicing must come from real resources (GDP growth, future surpluses), not perpetual borrowing.

**Common mistake to avoid:** Do NOT say "debt must be zero in the limit" - that's worth -10 points according to the professor!

## Essay 2: Global Saving Glut ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê

### Question:
**"Explain the Global Saving Glut hypothesis. According to this view, why were US interest rates low in the 2000s? What is the evidence for or against this hypothesis?" (14 marks)**

### Why This Question?
Week 7 had 22 test/exam mentions, with 10+ specifically about Global Saving Glut. Professor said "definitely on the test."

### Model Answer (14 marks):

**The Hypothesis (4 marks):**
The Global Saving Glut (Bernanke, 2005) attributes low US interest rates in the 2000s to excessive saving in emerging markets:
- China: Export-led growth, undervalued currency, high saving rates
- Oil exporters: High oil prices (2000s) ‚Üí large CA surpluses ‚Üí petrodollar recycling
- Asian countries: Precautionary saving after 1997 Asian Financial Crisis

**Mechanism (4 marks):**
1. Emerging markets ran large CA surpluses ‚Üí capital outflows
2. Bought US assets (especially Treasury bonds) ‚Üí demand for US debt ‚Üë
3. US interest rates fell (r* ‚Üì)
4. Low r* enabled US households/firms to borrow cheaply
5. US CA deficit widened to match foreign surpluses (global CA sum = 0)

**Evidence Supporting (3 marks):**
‚úÖ Asian saving rates rose dramatically (especially China from 35% to 50% of GDP)
‚úÖ Emerging market CA surpluses widened significantly in 2000s
‚úÖ Timing matches: saving glut coincides with low US rates
‚úÖ Foreign holdings of US Treasuries soared

**Evidence Against / Alternative (Investment Drought) (2 marks):**
‚ùå Alternative explanation (Summers): Low r* from falling investment demand (secular stagnation)
- But data shows investment was relatively stable, not falling
- Saving increase more pronounced in data

**Conclusion (1 mark):**
Evidence strongly supports Saving Glut over Investment Drought. The hypothesis also explains the link to 2008 crisis (low r* ‚Üí search for yield ‚Üí excessive risk-taking).

## Essay 3: NIIP and Valuation Changes ‚≠ê‚≠ê

### Question:
**"Why might a country's Net International Investment Position (NIIP) not equal its accumulated current account balances? Explain with reference to the US NIIP puzzle. (14 marks)**

### Why This Question?
Professor said "This is going to be on the test for sure" when discussing valuation changes.

### Model Answer (14 marks):

**NIIP Definition (2 marks):**
NIIP = Foreign Assets - Foreign Liabilities
It measures a country's net wealth position vis-√†-vis the rest of the world.

**Two Sources of NIIP Changes (4 marks):**
1. **Current Account Flows:**
   - CA surplus ‚Üí accumulate foreign assets ‚Üí NIIP ‚Üë
   - CA deficit ‚Üí accumulate foreign liabilities ‚Üí NIIP ‚Üì

2. **Valuation Changes:**
   - Stock market price changes
   - Bond price changes
   - Exchange rate movements
   - These change the VALUE of existing assets/liabilities without any flow

**The US Puzzle (4 marks):**
During 2002-2007:
- US ran $3.9 trillion in CA deficits
- Expected: NIIP should fall by $3.9 trillion
- **Actual: NIIP improved by $80 billion! (+$4 trillion swing)**

How? **Positive valuation changes of ~$4 trillion offset the CA deficits.**

**Explanation (2 marks):**
- US holds foreign assets mainly in stocks/FDI (risky, high return)
- Foreigners hold US assets mainly in bonds/Treasuries (safe, low return)
- When global stock markets rose, US foreign assets appreciated more than US foreign liabilities
- "Exorbitant privilege" of dollar ‚Üí return differential

**Conclusion (2 marks):**
NIIP ‚â† Accumulated CA because valuation effects matter. The US benefits from composition of its foreign portfolio (holds equity, owes debt).

**Formula:**
```
Œî NIIP_t = CA_t + Valuation Changes_t
```

## Essay 4: Metzler Diagram Application ‚≠ê‚≠ê

### Question:
**"Using the Metzler diagram, analyze the effects of a temporary government spending increase on the current account. How would your answer differ if the spending increase were permanent?" (14 marks)**

### Why This Question?
Week 6 had 10 test mentions about Metzler diagram. Professor said "This will definitely be on the test."

### Model Answer (14 marks):

**Setup (2 marks):**
The Metzler diagram plots:
- Vertical axis: world interest rate r*
- Horizontal axis: current account CA
- Upward-sloping CA curve (higher r* ‚Üí higher saving ‚Üí better CA)

**Temporary Government Spending Increase (6 marks):**

*Draw diagram showing:*
1. Initial CA curve and equilibrium at r*‚ÇÄ, CA‚ÇÄ
2. G‚Üë (temporary) ‚Üí National saving falls: S = Y - C - G ‚Üì
3. CA = S - I ‚Üí CA worsens
4. **CA curve shifts LEFT**
5. At unchanged r*‚ÇÄ, new equilibrium CA‚ÇÅ < CA‚ÇÄ (deficit worsens)
6. Magnitude: Large effect because temporary (no offsetting consumption change)

**Intuition:** Higher gov't spending reduces resources available for private saving ‚Üí CA deteriorates.

**Permanent Government Spending Increase (4 marks):**

*If G‚Üë is permanent:*
1. Still shifts CA curve left, BUT smaller shift
2. Why? Ricardian equivalence logic: permanent G‚Üë ‚Üí future taxes ‚Üë
3. Households save more to prepare for higher future taxes
4. Partially offsets the direct effect of G‚Üë on national saving

**Net effect:** CA curve shifts left, but less than temporary case.

**Conclusion (2 marks):**
- Temporary G‚Üë: Large leftward CA shift
- Permanent G‚Üë: Smaller leftward CA shift
- In both cases, CA worsens, but magnitude differs

**Key distinction for test:** Temporary shocks have LARGER CA effects (no consumption smoothing offsets).

## Essay 5: Current Account Determinants ‚≠ê‚≠ê

### Question:
**"List and explain FIVE factors that determine a country's current account balance. For each factor, explain the direction of the effect." (14 marks)**

### Why This Question?
Week 7 explicitly reviewed CA determinants as test preparation. Professor: "These questions could appear on the test in various forms."

### Model Answer (14 marks - allocate ~2.8 marks per factor):

**Fundamental Relationship (1 mark):**
```
CA = S - I
```
Current account = National saving - Investment

**Factor 1: Temporary Income Increase (3 marks):**
- **Effect:** CA improves (surplus increases or deficit shrinks)
- **Mechanism:** Permanent income hypothesis ‚Üí save windfall ‚Üí S‚Üë ‚Üí CA‚Üë
- **Example:** Oil discovery country saves most of temporary oil revenue

**Factor 2: Anticipated Future Income (3 marks):**
- **Effect:** CA worsens (deficit increases)
- **Mechanism:** Borrow against future income to smooth consumption today ‚Üí S‚Üì ‚Üí CA‚Üì
- **Example:** Developing country with good growth prospects borrows internationally

**Factor 3: World Interest Rate Increase (r*‚Üë) (2 marks):**
- **Effect:** CA improves
- **Mechanism:** Substitution effect (log utility) ‚Üí save more ‚Üí S‚Üë, and investment falls ‚Üí I‚Üì ‚Üí CA‚Üë
- **Magnitude:** Large if intertemporal elasticity of substitution is high

**Factor 4: Government Spending Increase (2 marks):**
- **Effect:** CA worsens
- **Mechanism:** G‚Üë ‚Üí national saving S = Y - C - G falls ‚Üí CA‚Üì
- **Fiscal expansion ‚Üí CA deficit** (twin deficits)

**Factor 5: Patience Increase (Time Preference Œ≤‚Üë) (2 marks):**
- **Effect:** CA improves
- **Mechanism:** More patient ‚Üí value future more ‚Üí save more today ‚Üí S‚Üë ‚Üí CA‚Üë
- **Example:** Cultural shift toward higher saving rates

**Bonus Factor - Investment/Productivity Shock (1 mark for completeness):**
- Positive productivity shock ‚Üí I‚Üë ‚Üí CA worsens (if investment effect dominates saving effect)
- Temporary productivity shock ‚Üí CA may improve (save windfall)
- Permanent productivity shock ‚Üí small CA effect (consumption smoothing)

**Conclusion:** CA is determined by anything affecting national saving or investment. The fundamental identity CA = S - I is the key to analyzing all shocks.

## Essay 6: Temporary vs Permanent Shocks ‚≠ê

### Question:
**"Compare the effects of a temporary versus permanent productivity increase on the current account. Why do these effects differ in magnitude?" (14 marks)**

### Model Answer (14 marks):

**Temporary Productivity Shock (6 marks):**

Current period: A‚ÇÅ‚Üë, Future: A‚ÇÇ unchanged

**Saving effect:**
- Q‚ÇÅ‚Üë but Q‚ÇÇ unchanged
- Permanent income hypothesis: spread consumption increase over both periods
- Save most of Q‚ÇÅ increase ‚Üí S‚ÇÅ‚Üë (large)

**Investment effect:**
- Only temporary ‚Üí MPK returns to normal next period
- I‚ÇÅ‚Üë slightly (build some capital to use while A‚ÇÅ high)
- But effect is muted because shock is temporary

**Net CA effect:** CA‚ÇÅ improves significantly (S‚Üë effect dominates I‚Üë)

**Permanent Productivity Shock (6 marks):**

Both periods: A‚ÇÅ‚Üë AND A‚ÇÇ‚Üë

**Saving effect:**
- Q‚ÇÅ‚Üë and Q‚ÇÇ‚Üë permanently
- Permanent income hypothesis: consumption C rises proportionally to Q in both periods
- S‚ÇÅ = Y‚ÇÅ - C‚ÇÅ - G ‚Üí small increase (C rises a lot)

**Investment effect:**
- Permanent ‚ÜëMPK ‚Üí I‚ÇÅ‚Üë substantially (profitable to build capital stock permanently)
- Larger investment response than temporary case

**Net CA effect:** CA‚ÇÅ may worsen OR small change (I‚Üë effect may dominate S effect)

**Key Difference (2 marks):**
Temporary shocks ‚Üí large CA response (save the windfall)
Permanent shocks ‚Üí small CA response (consumption smoothing + investment)

**Consumption smoothing is the key:** Temporary income changes are saved, permanent changes are consumed.

## Essay 7: Small vs Large Economy ‚≠ê

### Question:
**"Explain the key difference between small open economy and large open economy models in terms of interest rate determination. How does this affect the analysis of policy changes?" (14 marks)**

### Model Answer (14 marks):

**Small Open Economy (4 marks):**
- **r* is exogenous** (taken as given from world markets)
- Country is too small to affect world interest rate
- **Example:** New Zealand, Denmark
- **Implication:** Policy changes affect CA but not r*
- Metzler diagram: r* is horizontal line, economy adjusts CA

**Large Open Economy (4 marks):**
- **r* is endogenous** (determined by global market clearing)
- Country's saving/investment decisions affect world interest rate
- **Example:** United States, China, Eurozone
- **Implication:** Policy changes affect both CA and r*
- Must account for feedback effects

**Feedback Effects in Large Economy (4 marks):**

**Example: Country A increases saving**

*Small economy case:*
- S‚Üë ‚Üí CA improves by full amount of S‚Üë
- r* unchanged ‚Üí no feedback

*Large economy case:*
1. Direct effect: S‚Üë ‚Üí CA_A shifts right (improvement)
2. **But:** S‚Üë globally ‚Üí r*‚Üì (more capital supplied)
3. Lower r* ‚Üí movement back along CA curve
4. **Net effect:** CA_A improves, but LESS than small economy case

**The feedback dampens the initial effect.**

**Policy Implications (2 marks):**
- Large economy must consider spillover effects on rest of world
- "Beggar-thy-neighbor" policies affect global r*
- Coordination problems: Both countries can't improve CA simultaneously (global sum = 0)

## Essay 8: Consumption Smoothing

### Question:
**"Explain the principle of consumption smoothing in the context of international macroeconomics. How does it help explain current account patterns?" (14 marks)**

### Model Answer (14 marks):

**Principle (4 marks):**
Rational households prefer stable consumption over time rather than volatile consumption that follows income fluctuations.

**Formal:** Utility is concave in consumption
```
U(C‚ÇÅ, C‚ÇÇ) = u(C‚ÇÅ) + Œ≤u(C‚ÇÇ)
where u''(C) < 0 ‚Üí diminishing marginal utility
```

**Implication:** Smooth consumption ‚Üí save windfalls, borrow against future income

**Temporary Income Changes (4 marks):**

**Positive shock (Q‚ÇÅ‚Üë, Q‚ÇÇ unchanged):**
- Don't increase C‚ÇÅ by full amount of Q‚ÇÅ‚Üë
- Instead, save most of windfall to spread over lifetime
- **CA‚ÇÅ improves** (running surplus, accumulating foreign assets)

**Example:** Norway's oil fund - save oil revenues for future generations

**Negative shock (Q‚ÇÅ‚Üì, Q‚ÇÇ unchanged):**
- Don't cut C‚ÇÅ by full amount of Q‚ÇÅ‚Üì
- Instead, borrow internationally to maintain consumption
- **CA‚ÇÅ worsens** (running deficit, accumulating foreign debt)

**Example:** Country facing temporary natural disaster borrows internationally

**Permanent Income Changes (3 marks):**

**Permanent increase (Q‚ÇÅ‚Üë, Q‚ÇÇ‚Üë):**
- "Permanent income" is higher
- Can safely increase consumption in all periods
- **CA‚ÇÅ change is small** (C rises with Q)

**Permanent income hypothesis:** Only *permanent* income changes affect consumption proportionally.

**Empirical Support (2 marks):**
‚úÖ CA deficits in developing countries (borrowing against future growth)
‚úÖ CA surpluses in aging societies (Japan - saving for retirement)
‚úÖ Oil-rich countries save windfalls (consumption smoothing across generations)

**Conclusion (1 mark):**
Consumption smoothing via international borrowing/lending is the fundamental driver of current account dynamics. It explains why CA ‚â† 0 is optimal.
